16.1. Definition#

The Dirac delta can be loosely thought of as a function on the real line which is zero everywhere except at the origin, where it is infinite,

(16.1)#\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \delta(x) = \begin{cases} \infty & x=0 \\ 0 & x\neq 0 \end{cases} \end{align}\end{split}\]

and which is also constrained to satisfy the identity

(16.2)#\[\begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \delta(x)\,\mathrm{d}x = 1 \end{align}\]

A better characterization consists in consdering the Dirac delta function as a generalized function. In the theory of distributions, a generalized function is considered not a function in itself but only about how it affects other functions when “integrated” against them. In keeping with this philosophy, to define the delta function properly, it is enough to say what the “integral” of the delta function is against a sufficiently “good” test function \(f\)

(16.3)#\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \int_{\mathcal{I}} f(x)\,\delta(x)\,\mathrm{d}x = \begin{cases} f(0) & 0\in\mathcal{I} \\ 0 & 0\notin\mathcal{I} \end{cases} \end{align}\end{split}\]

where \(\mathcal{I}\) is an open interval of the real axis. In this respect, the Dirac delta can be defined as the distributional derivative of the Heaviside step function

(16.4)#\[ \begin{align} \delta(x) = \dot{H}(x) \end{align} \]

where the dot \(\cdot\) stands for the first-order derivative and \(H\) is the Heaviside step function

(16.5)#\[\begin{split} \begin{align} H(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x \geq 0 \\ 0 & x<0 \end{cases} \end{align} \end{split}\]

Proof of

Let us consider the integration by parts of two function \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\)

(16.6)#\[\begin{align} \int_a^b f(x)\,\dot{g}(x)\,\mathrm{d}x = \left. f(x)\,g(x)\,\right|_a^b - \int_a^b \dot{f}(x)\,g(x)\,\mathrm{d}x \end{align}\]

and apply it to the case in which \(g(x)=H(x)\) and \(a<0<b\)

(16.7)#\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \int_a^b f(x)\,\delta(x)\,\mathrm{d}x &= \left.f(x)\,H(x)\,\right|_a^b - \int_a^b \dot{f}(x)\,H(x)\,\mathrm{d}x \\ &= f(b) - \int_0^b \dot{f}(x)\,\mathrm{d}x = f(b) - \left. f(x)\,\right|_0^b = f(0) \end{align}\end{split}\]

16.2. Dirac delta derivative#

The Dirac delta derivative is defined as follows

(16.8)#\[\begin{align} \int_a^b f(x)\,\delta'(x)\,\mathrm{d}x &= \left. f(x)\,\delta(x)\,\right|_a^b - \int_a^b \dot{f}(x)\,\delta(x)\,\mathrm{d}x = -\dot{f}(0) \end{align}\]

For higher-order derivatives one obtians

(16.9)#\[\begin{align} \int_a^b f(x)\,\delta^{(n)}(x)\,\mathrm{d}x &= (-1)^n\,f^{(n)}(0) \end{align}\]

16.3. Basic propetries#

The Dirac delta is symmetric

(16.10)#\[\begin{align} \delta(-x) = \delta(x) \end{align}\]

and the product of the Dirac delta with \(x\) is equal to zero

(16.11)#\[\begin{align} x\,\delta(x) = 0 \end{align}\]

The Dirac delta derivative satisfies a number of basic properties

(16.12)#\[\begin{split}\begin{align} & \dot{\delta}(-x) = -\dot{\delta}(x) \\ & x\,\dot{\delta}(x) = -\delta(x) \end{align}\end{split}\]

16.4. Three-dimensional Dirac delta#

The three-dimensional Dirac delta can be defined in the Cartesian reference frame as

(16.13)#\[ \begin{align} \delta(\mathbf{x}) = \delta(x_1)\,\delta(x_2)\,\delta(x_3) \end{align}\]

where \(\mathbf{x}\) is the vector position and \(x_j\) are its Cartesian coordinates

(16.14)#\[ \begin{align} \mathbf{x} = x_j\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}_j \end{align}\]

with \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}_j\) being the Cartesian unit vectors.

Similar to the one-dimensional case, eq. (16.3), it satify the following identity

(16.15)#\[\begin{split}\begin{align} \int_{\mathcal{V}} f(\mathbf{x})\,\delta(\mathbf{x})\,\mathrm{d}V = \begin{cases} f(\mathbf{0}) & \mathbf{0}\in\mathcal{V} \\ 0 & \mathbf{0}\notin\mathcal{V} \end{cases} \end{align}\end{split}\]

for any (scalar, vector or tensor) field \(f\) that is continuous and has continuous first order partial derivatives. Here, \(\mathcal{V}\subseteq\mathbb{R}^3\) is a open region of the three-dimensional space and \(dV=dx_1\,dx_2\,dx_3\) is the infinitesimal volume element.

16.4.1. The Laplacian of the inverse of the radial distance#

Tip

To understand the content of this section, you should have been studied the Prerequisits

Let us consider the radial distance from the origin

(16.16)#\[ \begin{align} r = \sqrt{\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{x}} \end{align}\]

It can be shown that the Laplacian of the inverse of the radial distance is proportional to the three-dimensional Dirac delta. In particular, we can write

(16.17)#\[ \begin{align} \nabla^2 a(r) = \delta(\mathbf{x}) \end{align}\]

with \(a\) being the following function proportional to the inverse of the radial distance

(16.18)#\[ \begin{align} a(r) = -\frac{1}{4\,\pi\,r} \end{align}\]

First of all, let us consider that

\[ \begin{align} \nabla^2 a(r)= 0 \end{align}\]

everywhere but at the origin \(\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{0}\), where the function \(a\) is singular. In this respect, as far as the region \(\mathcal{V}\) does not contain the origin, the following integral yields always zero

\[ \int_\mathcal{V} f(\mathbf{x})\,\nabla^2 a(r)\,d V = 0 \qquad \mathbf{0}\notin\mathcal{V} \]

as expected from eq. (16.15).

In order to better understand the behaviour at the origin, let us now consider the following integral over an open volume \(\mathcal{V}\) containing the origin, \(\mathbf{0}\in\mathcal{V}\),

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} \int_{\mathcal{V}} \nabla^2 a(r)\,d V &= -\frac{1}{4\,\pi}\,\int_{\mathcal{B}}\nabla^2\frac{1}{r}\,d V = -\frac{1}{4\,\pi}\,\int_{\partial\mathcal{B}}\hat{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\boldsymbol{\nabla}\frac{1}{r}\,d S \\ &= \frac{1}{4\,\pi\,R^2}\,\int_{\partial \mathcal{B}} d S = 1 & \end{align} \end{split}\]

Here, \(\mathcal{B}\subset \mathcal{V}\) is a sphere of radius \(R\) centered at the origin, \(\partial \mathcal{B}\) is its boundary of area \(4\,\pi\,R^2\) and we made use of the divergence theorem to relate the volume and surface integrals. As expected setting \(f(\mathbf{x})=1\) in eq. (16.15), the integral yields \(1\).

In order to prove eq. (16.17), we have still to verify it for the case of \(\mathbf{0}\in\mathcal{V}\) and for any (scalar, vector or tensor) field \(f\). By making use of the following general identity

\[ \begin{align} \boldsymbol{\nabla}\cdot\big((\boldsymbol{\nabla}a(\mathbf{x}))\,f(\mathbf{x})\big) &= (\nabla^2 a(\mathbf{x}))\,f(\mathbf{x}) + \big(\boldsymbol{\nabla} a(\mathbf{x})\big)\cdot\big(\boldsymbol{\nabla} f(\mathbf{x}) \big) & \end{align}\]

for \(a\) given by eq. (16.18), we can write

\[ \begin{align} (\nabla^2 a(r))\,f(\mathbf{x}) &= \frac{1}{4\,\pi}\,\left[\boldsymbol{\nabla}\cdot\left(\frac{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2}\,f(\mathbf{x})\right) - \frac{1}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial f(\mathbf{x})}{\partial r}\right] & \end{align}\]

Then, by considering the desired integral and proceed as follows

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} \int_\mathcal{V} f(\mathbf{x})\,\nabla^2 a(r)\,d V &= \int_\mathcal{B} f(\mathbf{x})\,\nabla^2 a(r)\,d V = \frac{1}{4\,\pi}\,\left(\int_{\partial\mathcal{B}} \frac{f(\mathbf{x})}{r^2}\,d S - \int_{\mathcal{B}} \frac{1}{r^2}\,\frac{\partial f(\mathbf{x})}{\partial r}\,d V \right)& \\ & = \frac{1}{4\,\pi}\,\left(\int_{\Omega}\,f(\mathbf{x})|_{r=R}\,d \Omega - \int_{\Omega}\int_0^R \frac{\partial f(\mathbf{x})}{\partial r}\,d r\,d \Omega \right) \\ &= \frac{1}{4\,\pi}\,\left(\int_{\Omega}\,f(\mathbf{x})|_{r=R}\,d \Omega - \int_{\Omega}\Big(f(\mathbf{x})|_{r=R}-f(\mathbf{x})|_{r=0}\Big)\,d \Omega \right) \\ & = \frac{1}{4\,\pi}\,\int_{\Omega}f(\mathbf{0})\,d \Omega = f(\mathbf{0}) \qquad \mathbf{0}\in\mathcal{V} \end{align} \end{split}\]

we can see that, as expected, it yields the field evaluated at the origin when \(\mathbf{0}\in\mathcal{V}\).
